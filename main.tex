\section{Introduction}

Options are widely used in financial markets for hedging, speculation, and risk management. A central challenge in quantitative finance is managing option risk through dynamic hedging. The Black–Scholes framework provides the theoretical foundation for this problem, under assumptions of continuous trading and ideal conditions. However, markets are characterized by discrete trading, transaction costs, and time-varying volatility. These deviations make perfect replication impossible and introduce hedging error. Understanding and quantifying this error is essential.

This study performs a dynamic hedging for European options that bridges theory and real-world implementation. It evaluates hedging performance under discrete rebalancing, transaction costs. The framework compares multiple strategies across rebalancing frequencies and benchmarks them against no-hedge and static hedge approaches. To analyze hedging error, we use two complementary tools: the Bertsimas–Kogan–Lo (BKL) approximation; which quantifies discretization-induced variance as a baseline for hedging error variance and a Greek-based P\&L attribution. The results show that real-world hedging performance depends on a combination of discretization effects, transaction costs, and volatility dynamics, underscoring the limitations of idealized models.
\section{Background}
\subsection{ Conditional Value at Risk (CVar)}

To quantify tail risk in the dynamic hedging strategy, we employ CVaR. Unlike VaR, which is not a coherent risk measure, CVaR provides a mathematically consistent and robust characterization of extreme losses. This makes it particularly suitable for evaluating the risk of discrete hedging strategies, where large deviations may arise due to market frictions and rebalancing constraints.

Let $L$ denote a continuous random variable representing the loss of the delta-hedging portfolio. 
\[
L = - \big( V_{\text{portfolio}} - V_{\text{option}} \big),
\]
where $V_{\text{portfolio}}$ is the value of the replicating portfolio and $V_{\text{option}}$ is the theoretical option price.

Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ be a probability space and let $F_L(l) = \mathbb{P}(L \leq l)$ denote the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of $L$. For a confidence level $\alpha \in (0,1)$ the VaR is defined as the $\alpha$-quantile of the loss distribution:
\[
\text{VaR}_\alpha(L) = \inf \{ l \in \mathbb{R} \mid F_L(l) \geq \alpha \}.
\]

The CVaR is defined as the expected loss conditional on exceeding the VaR threshold. For continuous distributions:
\[
\text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \mathbb{E} \left[ L \mid L \geq \text{VaR}_\alpha(L) \right].
\]

% \[

% \]

% If $L$ admits a continuous density function $f_L(l)$, this reduces to a tail expectation:
% \[
% \text{CVaR}_\alpha(L) = \frac{1}{1 - \alpha} 
% \int_{\text{VaR}_\alpha(L)}^{\infty} l \, f_L(l) \, dl.
% \]

In a continuous-time setting, perfect replication is theoretically achievable under ideal assumptions. However, in practice, hedging occurs at discrete intervals $\Delta t$, introducing tracking error and transaction costs.

To balance tail risk against trading costs, we define the following objective function:
\[
J(\Delta t) = \max\Big(0, \, \text{CVaR}_\alpha\big(L(\Delta t)\big)\Big) 
+ \mathbb{E}\big[\text{TC}(\Delta t)\big],
\]
where $\text{TC}(\Delta t)$ denotes the cumulative transaction costs associated with the hedging strategy.

This formulation penalizes extreme losses through CVaR while incorporating the economic cost of rebalancing. As a result, it provides a tractable discrete strategy-selection framework for determining an optimal hedging frequency that balances risk reduction against market frictions.

\subsection{The Bertsimas--Kogan--Lo (BKL) }
The classical BlackScholes framework assumes continuous-time trading, under which a derivative can be perfectly replicated by dynamically adjusting a self-financing portfolio. In this idealized setting, continuous rebalancing ($\Delta t \to 0$) ensures that the replication error is identically zero. However, trading occurs at discrete time intervals. This introduces \emph{discretization error}, as the hedging portfolio is only updated intermittently while the underlying asset evolves continuously. The BKL framework provides a mathematical foundation to quantify this effect by deriving an explicit approximation for the variance of the discrete hedging error.

Delta hedging neutralizes the first-order sensitivity of the option price with respect to the underlying asset. However, the option remains exposed to its second-order sensitivity, known as Gamma ($\Gamma$). Between rebalancing times, the underlying asset price changes randomly, and the linear hedging portfolio cannot capture the curvature of the option payoff. This mismatch generates a residual tracking error. The primary utility of the BKL approximation is characterizing the expected variance of this Gamma-driven residual error across discrete time steps.


Let $S_t$ denote the underlying asset price following a geometric Brownian motion with volatility $\sigma$, and let $V(t,S_t)$ denote the option price. Over a small time interval $\Delta t$, a second-order expansion yields:
\[
\Delta V_t \approx \Theta_t \Delta t + \Delta_t \Delta S_t + \frac{1}{2} \Gamma_t (\Delta S_t)^2.
\]

A delta-hedged portfolio eliminates the linear term $\Delta_t \Delta S_t$, leaving a residual error driven by Gamma. Using the BS partial differential equation, the one-step hedging error can be approximated as:
\[
e_t \approx \frac{1}{2} \Gamma_t \left[ (\Delta S_t)^2 - \sigma^2 S_t^2 \Delta t \right].
\]

Expressing the stock increment in terms of a Wiener process ($\Delta W_t$), this becomes:
\[
e_t \approx \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S_t^2 \Gamma_t \left( (\Delta W_t)^2 - \Delta t \right).
\]

Although the expected value of the local error is zero, its variance is strictly positive. The BKL approximation shows that the variance of the single-step hedging error is:
\[
\text{Var}(e_t) = \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma^2 S_t^2 \Gamma_t \right)^2 (\Delta t)^2.
\]

Aggregating over $N$ discrete rebalancing intervals up to maturity $T$, the total hedging error variance is:
\[
\text{Var}_{\text{Total}} \approx \sum_{i=1}^{N} 
\frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma^2 S_{t_i}^2 \Gamma_{t_i} \right)^2 (\Delta t)^2.
\]

\section{Experimental details}
This study develops a dynamic, self-financing delta-hedging simulation framework for European options under discrete trading constraints. The objective is to quantify replication error, attribute its sources, and evaluate optimal hedging frequency under transaction costs and tail-risk considerations. The framework is implemented across two distinct environments: a synthetic BS baseline for theoretical validation and a real-market pipeline utilizing historical options and equity data.

\subsection{Experimental Environments}

\subsubsection{Empirical Market Implementation}
To test the methodology sequentially in non-stationary regimes, the empirical framework integrates true historical asset prices and traded option chains from Yahoo Finance.

Options are selected dynamically based on adherence to rigorous liquidity and moneyness constraints:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Maturity Constraint:} Contracts must have $\geq 30$ calendar days to expiration.
    \item \textbf{Moneyness and Delta Filters:} Strike prices are strictly restricted to within $\pm 10\%$ of the prevailing spot price, prioritizing contracts whose absolute Black--Scholes Delta ($|\Delta|$) is between $0.25$ and $0.75$, effectively targeting near-the-money derivatives.
    \item \textbf{Liquidity Threshold:} Selected contracts must exhibit strictly positive open interest.
\end{itemize}

In real markets, implied volatility is often unavailable or heavily illiquid. To maintain consistency across the timeline, when implied volatility falls below a $5\%$ algorithmic boundary, the framework defaults to a 21-day rolling historical realized volatility estimator. Option and underlying time series are synchronized via inner joins on daily timestamps, computing time-to-maturity ($\tau$) as the annualized calendar fraction $\tau = (T - t) / 365$.

\subsection{Pricing and Portfolio Dynamics}

Option valuations and associated Greeks are computed sequentially under the standard BS framework. The core sensitivity, Delta ($\Delta$), dictates the theoretical share position required for replication, where $\Delta = e^{-q\tau}\Phi(d_1)$. 

The strategy mimics the behavior of an option writer establishing a locally risk-neutral, self-financing replicating portfolio. At inception $t_0$, the cash account absorbs the theoretical option premium:
\[
\text{Cash}_{t_0} = V_{\text{option}}(t_0).
\]

At any discrete rebalancing interval $t$, the portfolio is aligned to the target Delta position ($\Delta_{\text{target}} = \Delta_t$). The transaction cost (TC) incurred at $t$ is strictly proportional to the rebalancing volume:
\[
\text{TC}_t = |\Delta_t - \Delta_{t-1}| \times S_t \times c,
\]
where $c = 0.0005$ dictates the 5 basis-point friction.

Between intervals, the uninvested cash balance accrues risk-free interest. Accounting for trade execution and transaction costs, the cash balance evolves recursively as:
\[
\text{Cash}_t = \text{Cash}_{t-1} e^{r \Delta t} - (\Delta_t - \Delta_{t-1}) S_t - \text{TC}_t.
\]

The marked-to-market value of the tracking portfolio is thus $V_{\text{portfolio}, t} = \Delta_t S_t + \text{Cash}_t$, yielding an instantaneous discrete hedging error defined as:
\[
e_t = V_{\text{portfolio},t} - V_{\text{option},t}.
\]

\subsection{Hedging Strategies and Optimization Objective}

Hedging profiles are evaluated across four structured paradigms:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Periodic Hedging:} The target Delta is realigned at fixed intervals of 1, 2, or 5 trading days.
    \item \textbf{Static Hedging:} A strict buy-and-hold methodology where the initial $\Delta_0$ is held until maturity without further rebalancing.
    \item \textbf{No Hedge:} An unhedged benchmark corresponding to a premium-only cash balance maintained against the option liability.
    \item \textbf{Continuous Benchmark:} An idealized limit serving as a benchmark against tracking error constraints.
\end{enumerate}

To isolate the optimal discrete frequency, strategies are scored heavily on a strategy-selection objective function weighing severe replication failure against accumulated transaction costs:
\[
J(\Delta t) = \max(0, -\text{CVaR}_{95}(L)) + \sum \text{TC}_t,
\]
where the loss mapping $L = -e_t$ quantifies the replication deficit, and $\text{CVaR}_{95}(L) = \mathbb{E}[L \mid L \geq \text{VaR}_{95}]$ captures the 95th-percentile expected tail failure.

\subsection{Greek and Variance Attribution}

To diagnose sources of hedging divergence, vital when migrating from the synthetic environment to empirical data, the discrete P\&L is evaluated via two complementary lenses.

First, evaluating tracking variance using the BKL approximation defines the theoretical floor corresponding exclusively to the spacing of temporal rebalancing:
\[
\text{Var}(e_t) \approx \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma^2 S_t^2 \Gamma_t \right)^2 (\Delta t)^2.
\]

Second, a full Greek attribution decomposes observed aggregate P\&L perturbations into their generating fundamental vectors:
\begin{align*}
\Delta \text{PnL}_{\Delta} &= \Delta_{t-1}(S_t - S_{t-1}), \\
\Delta \text{PnL}_{\Gamma} &= \frac{1}{2}\Gamma_{t-1}(S_t - S_{t-1})^2, \\
\Delta \text{PnL}_{\Theta} &= \Theta_{t-1}(\tau_{t-1} - \tau_t), \\
\Delta \text{PnL}_{\mathcal{V}} &= \mathcal{V}_{t-1}(\sigma_t - \sigma_{t-1}), \\
\Delta \text{PnL}_{\rho} &= \rho_{t-1}(r_t - r_{t-1}).
\end{align*}

\subsection{Volatility Mismatch Experiment}
Under idealized assumptions, daily rebalancing is sufficient to mitigate discretization errors according to the BKL framework. However, theoretical models frequently fail upon misspecification of the volatility parameter ($\sigma$). 

To isolate and rigorously penalize tracking error decoupled from frequency artifacts, a dedicated volatility mismatch assay is executed under a daily-rebalancing ($\Delta t = 1$) envelope applying two disjoint structural inputs:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fixed Volatility Baseline:} Prices and Greeks are computed exclusively referencing a static initial value of $\sigma = 0.20$.
    \item \textbf{Realized Volatility Baseline:} Parameters dynamically track a backward-looking 21-day realized volatility window:
    \[
    \sigma_t = \sqrt{252} \cdot \text{StdDev} \left( \ln \left( \frac{S_{t-i}}{S_{t-i-1}} \right) \right)_{i=1}^{21}.
    \]
\end{enumerate}

Simulating these tracks in parallel isolates tracking deviations purely attributable to structural parameter mismatches, translating directly into residual $\Gamma$ and $\mathcal{V}$ artifacts over identical market events. This assesses the model's tolerance limits when constrained assumptions fundamentally detach from realized distributional physics.
\section{Results}


The performance of the dynamic delta-hedging framework is evaluated across two environments: a controlled synthetic Black--Scholes setting and an empirical implementation using real-world options data.

\subsection{Theoretical Baseline Results}

Under the Black--Scholes assumptions of constant volatility ($\sigma = 0.20$) and risk-free rate ($r = 0.02$), the hedging error behaves consistently with theoretical predictions.

The continuous-time benchmark yields zero replication error and zero transaction costs, confirming the validity of the implementation. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:hedging_summary}, for discrete hedging strategies, the final absolute hedging error increases monotonically with rebalancing interval:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Daily hedging: $\$2.38$
    \item Every 2 days: $\$2.68$
    \item Weekly (5 days): $\$3.60$
\end{itemize}

Benchmark strategies exhibit significantly larger deviations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item No hedge: $\$6.62$
    \item Static hedge: $\$4.79$
\end{itemize}

When accounting for transaction costs, the optimal strategy selected via CVaR minimization is the 5-day rebalancing policy, which achieves the lowest combined risk-cost trade-off.

\subsection{Empirical Results Using Market Data}

In the empirical setting, a near-delta-targeted SPY call (SPY260618C00730000, Strike: \$730) within the moneyness filter was analyzed using historical data. Due to unreliable implied volatility data, a 21-day rolling realized volatility estimator was employed.

The results deviate significantly from the theoretical baseline, as quantified in Table~\ref{tab:hedging_summary_empirical}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The unhedged position incurs a large loss ($-\$9.70$), reflecting directional exposure.
    \item Delta-hedged strategies generate positive terminal values (e.g., $+\$5.83$ for daily hedging), indicating systematic over-hedging relative to the actual option payoff.
\end{itemize}

These outcomes demonstrate that model misspecification, particularly in volatility estimation, dominates discretization effects in real-world applications.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The results highlight three key insights:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Limits of Continuous Hedging:} Continuous replication is not achievable in practice. Transaction costs impose an optimal hedging frequency.
    
    \item \textbf{Scope of BKL Approximation:} The BKL framework accurately captures discretization error but does not account for model misspecification.
    
    \item \textbf{Importance of Volatility Modeling:} Dynamic hedging is fundamentally sensitive to volatility assumptions. Errors in volatility estimation translate directly into systematic hedging losses or gains.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, the findings demonstrate that while Black--Scholes provides a useful theoretical foundation, real-world hedging performance is governed by a combination of discretization effects, transaction costs, and model risk.

\section{Conclusion}

This study develops and evaluates a dynamic delta-hedging framework under realistic trading constraints, bridging the gap between the theoretical ideal of continuous-time replication and the practical limitations of discrete financial markets. By integrating Black--Scholes pricing, the Bertsimas--Kogan--Lo (BKL) approximation, and Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR)-based optimization, the framework provides a unified approach to analyzing hedging performance, transaction costs, and tail risk.

In the synthetic Black--Scholes environment, the results confirm classical theoretical predictions. As the rebalancing interval decreases, replication error converges toward zero, and the observed variance closely matches the BKL approximation. This validates both the numerical implementation and the theoretical relationship between discretization error and hedging frequency. However, once transaction costs are incorporated, continuous or high-frequency hedging is no longer optimal. Instead, a lower-frequency strategy (5-day rebalancing) emerges as optimal under a CVaR-based objective in both theoretical and empirical domains, demonstrating the practical trade-off between hedging precision tail risks and trading frictions.

The empirical analysis using real market data reveals a more nuanced picture. In contrast to the synthetic setting, hedging performance is dominated not by discretization error, but by model misspecification—particularly in volatility estimation. The use of realized volatility as a proxy for implied volatility leads to systematic deviations in hedge ratios, resulting in large and persistent replication errors. These findings are further reinforced by the volatility mismatch experiment, which isolates the impact of incorrect volatility assumptions and demonstrates that even with continuous rebalancing, significant hedging error remains unavoidable when model inputs are misaligned with market dynamics.

The BKL approximation, while highly effective in bounding discretization-induced variance, is shown to be limited in scope. It does not account for parameter uncertainty or market microstructure effects, and therefore cannot fully explain empirical hedging outcomes. Similarly, the Greek P\&L attribution analysis highlights that real-world deviations are driven not only by Gamma-related discretization effects, but also by unhedged Vega exposure and time-varying volatility.

Overall, the results emphasize that dynamic hedging is not merely a mechanical application of delta-neutral replication, but an inherently model-dependent process. In practice, hedging strategies implicitly encode assumptions about future volatility, and their performance is critically sensitive to the accuracy of these assumptions.

Future work may extend this framework by incorporating stochastic volatility models, transaction cost optimization under liquidity constraints, and machine learning-based volatility forecasting. Such extensions would further enhance the robustness of hedging strategies in complex and evolving market environments.

In conclusion, while the Black--Scholes model provides a foundational benchmark for option replication, effective real-world hedging requires careful consideration of discretization effects, transaction costs, and, most importantly, model risk arising from volatility dynamics.

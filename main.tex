\section{Introduction}

Options are widely used in financial markets for hedging, speculation, and risk management. A central challenge in quantitative finance is managing option risk through dynamic hedging. The Black–Scholes framework provides the theoretical foundation for this problem, under assumptions of continuous trading and ideal conditions. Understanding and quantifying this error is essential.

This study performs a dynamic hedging for European options that bridges theory and real-world implementation. It evaluates hedging performance under discrete rebalancing, transaction costs. The framework compares multiple strategies across rebalancing frequencies and benchmarks them against no-hedge and static hedge approaches. To analyze hedging error, we use two complementary tools: the Bertsimas–Kogan–Lo (BKL) approximation; which quantifies discretization-induced variance as a baseline for hedging error variance and a Greek-based P\&L attribution. The results show that real-world hedging performance depends on a combination of discretization effects, transaction costs, and volatility dynamics, underscoring the limitations of idealized models.

In a continuous-time setting, perfect replication is theoretically achievable under ideal assumptions. However, in practice, hedging occurs at discrete intervals $\Delta t$, introducing tracking error and transaction costs.

To balance tail risk against trading costs, we define the following objective function:
\[
J(\Delta t) = \text{CVaR}_\alpha\big(L(\Delta t)\big)
+ \mathbb{E}\big[\text{TC}(\Delta t)\big],
\]
where $\text{TC}(\Delta t)$ denotes the cumulative transaction costs associated with the hedging strategy.

This formulation penalizes extreme losses through CVaR while incorporating the economic cost of rebalancing. As a result, it provides a tractable discrete strategy-selection framework for determining an optimal hedging frequency that balances risk reduction against market frictions.

\subsection{The Bertsimas--Kogan--Lo (BKL) }
The classical BlackScholes framework assumes continuous-time trading, under which a derivative can be perfectly replicated by dynamically adjusting a self-financing portfolio. In this idealized setting, continuous rebalancing ($\Delta t \to 0$) ensures that the replication error is identically zero. However, trading occurs at discrete time intervals. This introduces \emph{discretization error}, as the hedging portfolio is only updated intermittently while the underlying asset evolves continuously. The BKL framework provides a mathematical foundation to quantify this effect by deriving an explicit approximation for the variance of the discrete hedging error.

Delta hedging neutralizes the first-order sensitivity of the option price with respect to the underlying asset. However, the option remains exposed to its second-order sensitivity, known as Gamma ($\Gamma$). \[
\Delta V_t \approx \Theta_t \Delta t + \Delta_t \Delta S_t + \frac{1}{2} \Gamma_t (\Delta S_t)^2.
\]

A delta-hedged portfolio eliminates the linear term $\Delta_t \Delta S_t$, leaving a residual error driven by Gamma. Using the BS partial differential equation, the one-step hedging error can be approximated as:
\[
e_t \approx \frac{1}{2} \Gamma_t \left[ (\Delta S_t)^2 - \sigma^2 S_t^2 \Delta t \right].
\]

Expressing the stock increment in terms of a Wiener process ($\Delta W_t$), this becomes:
\[
e_t \approx \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S_t^2 \Gamma_t \left( (\Delta W_t)^2 - \Delta t \right).
\]

Although the expected value of the local error is zero, its variance is strictly positive. The BKL approximation shows that the variance of the single-step hedging error is:
\[
\text{Var}(e_t) = \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma^2 S_t^2 \Gamma_t \right)^2 (\Delta t)^2.
\]

\frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma^2 S_{t_i}^2 \Gamma_{t_i} \right)^2 (\Delta t)^2.

\section{Experimental details}
This study develops a dynamic, self-financing delta-hedging simulation framework for European options under discrete trading constraints. The objective is to quantify replication error, attribute its sources, and evaluate optimal hedging frequency under transaction costs and tail-risk considerations. The framework is implemented across two distinct environments: a historical-path Black--Scholes benchmark for theoretical validation and a real-market pipeline utilizing historical options and equity data.

\subsection{Volatility Mismatch Experiment}
Under idealized assumptions, daily rebalancing is sufficient to mitigate discretization errors according to the BKL framework. However, theoretical models frequently fail upon misspecification of the volatility parameter ($\sigma$). 

To isolate and rigorously penalize tracking error decoupled from frequency artifacts, a dedicated volatility mismatch assay is executed under a daily-rebalancing ($\Delta t = 1$) envelope applying two disjoint structural inputs:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fixed Volatility Baseline:} Prices and Greeks are computed exclusively referencing a static initial value of $\sigma = 0.20$.
    \item \textbf{Realized Volatility Baseline:} Parameters dynamically track a backward-looking 21-day realized volatility window:
    \[
    \sigma_t = \sqrt{252} \cdot \text{StdDev} \left( \ln \left( \frac{S_{t-i}}{S_{t-i-1}} \right) \right)_{i=1}^{21}.
    \]
\end{enumerate}

Simulating these tracks in parallel isolates tracking deviations purely attributable to structural parameter mismatches, translating directly into residual $\Gamma$ and $\mathcal{V}$ artifacts over identical market events. This assesses the model's tolerance limits when constrained assumptions fundamentally detach from realized distributional physics.

\section{Results}


The performance of the dynamic delta-hedging framework is evaluated across two environments: a historical-path Black--Scholes benchmark and an illustrative empirical implementation using real-world options data.

\subsection{Historical-Path Black--Scholes Benchmark Results}

Under the Black--Scholes assumptions of constant volatility ($\sigma = 0.20$) and risk-free rate ($r = 0.02$), the benchmark hedge errors behave consistently with the expected direction of the discrete-hedging trade-off.

The continuous-time benchmark yields zero replication error and zero transaction costs, confirming the validity of the replication accounting. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:hedging_summary}, for discrete hedging strategies, the end-of-sample absolute hedging error increases with rebalancing interval:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Daily hedging: $\$2.38$
    \item Every 2 days: $\$2.68$
    \item Weekly (5 days): $\$3.60$
\end{itemize}

Benchmark strategies exhibit significantly larger deviations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item No hedge: $\$6.62$
    \item No hedge: $\$6.62$
    \item Static hedge: $\$4.79$
\end{itemize}

When accounting for transaction costs, the optimal strategy selected via hedge-loss CVaR minimization is the 5-day rebalancing policy, which achieves the lowest combined risk-cost trade-off.

\subsection{Empirical Results Using Market Data}

In the empirical setting, the Yahoo Finance pipeline was run on a fixed set of SPY call options rather than resampling a single contract. Three representative contracts are reported as an illustrative case study because they span different expirations, strikes, history lengths, model deltas, and selected rebalancing policies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item SPY260618C00725000: June 18, 2026 expiry, strike \$725, 312 observations, estimated absolute delta 0.573.
    \item SPY260630C00730000: June 30, 2026 expiry, strike \$730, 175 observations, estimated absolute delta 0.452.
    \item SPY260717C00730000: July 17, 2026 expiry, strike \$730, 37 observations, estimated absolute delta 0.492.
\end{itemize}

The results deviate significantly from the historical-path benchmark, as quantified in Tables~\ref{tab:yahoo_contract_summary} and~\ref{tab:hedging_summary_empirical}. The three contracts do not select the same rebalancing interval:
\begin{itemize}
    \item SPY260618C00725000 selects every 5 days, with end-of-sample hedge error \$15.32, RMSE \$10.72, CVaR$_{95}$ hedge loss \$0.42, transaction cost \$0.71, and objective value \$1.13.
    \item SPY260630C00730000 selects every 2 days, with end-of-sample hedge error \$7.16, RMSE \$4.80, CVaR$_{95}$ hedge loss \$0.47, transaction cost \$0.89, and objective value \$1.36.
    \item SPY260717C00730000 selects every 2 days, with end-of-sample hedge error \$0.33, RMSE \$1.17, CVaR$_{95}$ hedge loss \$2.38, transaction cost \$0.32, and objective value \$2.70.
\end{itemize}

These outcomes suggest that empirical hedging performance is contract-specific within this small Yahoo case study. The June 18 contract favors lower transaction costs from 5-day rebalancing, while the June 30 and July 17 contracts favor 2-day rebalancing under the hedge-loss CVaR objective. Because the sample contains only a few SPY calls, these results should be interpreted as illustrative evidence rather than a broad empirical statement about optimal hedging frequency.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Discrete Hedging and Theoretical Validation}

In the historical-path Black--Scholes benchmark, results align with the expected direction of the discrete-hedging trade-off. As the rebalancing interval decreases, hedging error generally moves closer to the continuous-time limit of the Black--Scholes model, while transaction costs increase.

Figure~\ref{fig:bkl_comparison} compares realized hedge-error dispersion with the Bertsimas--Kogan--Lo (BKL) approximation. In the benchmark domain, the BKL estimate provides a useful theoretical scale for discretization error and now varies with the actual rebalancing interval. In the empirical Yahoo runs, the realized hedge errors are not explained by BKL alone because the approximation captures only discrete rebalancing error, not volatility misspecification, liquidity effects, stale prices, or changes in implied volatility.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The results highlight three key insights:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Limits of Continuous Hedging:} Continuous replication is not achievable in practice. Transaction costs impose an optimal hedging frequency.
    
    \item \textbf{Scope of BKL Approximation:} The BKL framework accurately captures discretization error but does not account for model misspecification.
    
    \item \textbf{Importance of Volatility Modeling:} Dynamic hedging is fundamentally sensitive to volatility assumptions. Errors in volatility estimation translate directly into systematic hedging losses or gains.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, the findings demonstrate that while Black--Scholes provides a useful theoretical foundation, real-world hedging performance is governed by a combination of discretization effects, transaction costs, and model risk.

\section{Conclusion}

This study develops and evaluates a dynamic delta-hedging framework under realistic trading constraints. In the historical-path Black--Scholes benchmark, the results show the expected trade-off between replication accuracy and trading costs: more frequent rebalancing lowers the BKL discretization scale, while transaction costs make the 5-day strategy optimal under the CVaR-loss-plus-cost objective. The updated BKL calculation provides a theoretical scale for the discretization component of hedge-error variance and varies with the actual rebalancing interval.

The empirical analysis using real market data reveals a more nuanced picture. Across the three reported Yahoo SPY calls, the selected rebalancing frequency is contract-dependent: the June 18 \$725 call selects 5-day rebalancing, while the June 30 \$730 and July 17 \$730 calls select 2-day rebalancing. Because this is a small SPY call case study, these results are best interpreted as illustrative rather than as a market-wide estimate of optimal hedging frequency.

The BKL approximation is useful for isolating discretization-induced variance, but it cannot fully explain empirical hedging outcomes because it does not account for parameter uncertainty, liquidity, stale option prices, bid--ask effects, or market microstructure. Similarly, the Greek P\&L attribution analysis highlights that real-world deviations are driven not only by Gamma-related discretization effects, but also by Vega exposure and time-varying volatility.

Overall, the results emphasize that dynamic hedging is not merely a mechanical application of delta-neutral replication, but an inherently model-dependent process. In practice, hedging strategies implicitly encode assumptions about future volatility, and their performance is critically sensitive to the accuracy of these assumptions.
